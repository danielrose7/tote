# Practical Tastemaker GTM

Working plan for focusing Tote around a single primary user persona and a clearer template strategy.

## Core Decision

Tote's beachhead persona is the **practical tastemaker**.

This is not primarily an affiliate marketer, professional creator, or generic online shopper. It is the person whose friends, family, partner, coworkers, or small audience already ask:

> Where did you find that?

They make useful shopping lists because they have taste, do research, and like helping people choose well.

## Persona

### Primary User

The practical tastemaker:

- Shops across many stores, not just one marketplace
- Saves products before the final decision is clear
- Has opinions about quality, usefulness, taste, and context
- Makes lists for real people and real situations
- Shares links with friends, family, partners, group chats, readers, or clients
- Wants a list to feel polished and sendable without becoming content sludge
- May have a small audience, but does not need one

Examples:

- The sibling who researches baby gear before anyone else
- The friend who always finds the good housewarming gift
- The parent who makes the shared holiday list
- The home/style person who sends friends considered edits
- The Substack-adjacent writer who occasionally wants a shoppable list
- The coworker everyone asks before buying kitchen gear, travel gear, or clothes

### Secondary User

Small creators and newsletter writers who make product recommendations can use Tote, but the product should not initially orient around affiliate workflows.

Affiliate users pull the product toward:

- Commission tracking
- Link management
- Disclosure tooling
- Brand deals
- Audience analytics
- Creator dashboards

Those may become later features, but they should not define the first wedge.

## Positioning

### Hook Principle

Sell the vacation, not the suitcase.

The hook should not lead with features like saving, organizing, syncing, templates, slots, or collections. Those are supporting mechanics.

The hook should sell the outcome:

- You become the person with the good list.
- The group chat gets one useful link instead of scattered suggestions.
- The gift giver feels relieved.
- The new parent feels less buried in gear research.
- The friend opening the link thinks, "Oh, this is exactly what I needed."

Lead with the feeling of usefulness, taste, relief, and social generosity. Explain the product only after the person wants the outcome.

### Short Version

Tote helps practical tastemakers make useful shopping lists people actually want to receive.

### Product Frame

Less:

> Organize products from every store.

More:

> Make and share considered shopping lists from across the internet.

Even better:

> Be the person with the list everyone actually uses.

### Why This Is Stronger

The public list is the growth object.

Someone can land on a useful public collection, trust the taste, copy the structure, and make their own. This gives Tote a social loop that a private shopping organizer does not have by itself.

## GTM Loops

### 1. Browse Loop

Someone discovers a public collection from search, social, or a friend.

They browse the list first, then understand Tote second.

Success signal:

- Public collection page views
- Clicks from use-case pages to public collections
- Return visits to `/templates`

### 2. Copy Loop

Someone finds a list close to their real situation and starts their own version.

Success signal:

- Clone/copy starts
- Signups from public collection pages
- Extension installs after viewing a template

### 3. Share Loop

Someone makes a list for a person, group, event, home, trip, or project and sends it.

Success signal:

- Public collection shares
- Public collection page views from direct/referral traffic
- Multiple viewers per public collection

## Template Strategy

Templates should feel like **socially useful edits**, not generic shopping categories.

The litmus test:

> Would someone send this to a friend with "this is actually useful"?

If yes, it fits the strategy.

If it only exists because a keyword has volume, it probably needs a sharper point of view.

## Template Buckets

### 1. Sendable Lists

Highest priority. These naturally move through texts, group chats, family threads, and DMs.

Examples:

- Baby shower gifts for the person actually having the baby
- Gifts for dads who hate being asked
- Housewarming gifts worth giving
- Kitchen gifts for someone who cooks on weeknights
- A starter registry that lasts past the newborn stage
- Back-to-school dorm things worth bringing
- Gifts under $50 for coworkers

### 2. Personal Planning Lists

Useful for one person or household making a practical decision.

Examples:

- Small-space baby gear that earns its floor space
- New apartment things you only want to buy once
- Home office setup under $1,500
- Carry-on only: 10 days in Europe
- Capsule wardrobe for working from home
- First dinner party kit

### 3. Public Taste Edits

More editorial, more Pinterest/Substack-adjacent. These should still be practical and shoppable.

Examples:

- Things worth buying before hosting
- Tools for a considered gardener
- Everyday wardrobe rotation, supply-chain honest
- A calm nursery edit for the first three months
- Host gifts that are not candles

## Use-Case Strategy

Use-case pages should support the practical tastemaker frame.

They should not read like broad product explainers. Each page should show a real list-making behavior:

- Make a gift list people can actually use
- Make one list for the group chat
- Turn home research into a shareable shortlist
- Build a style board that stays shoppable
- Make a public edit from products you would actually recommend

De-emphasize professional/client project positioning until there are stronger signs that pro sourcing is pulling users in.

## Distribution

Market the lists, not the app.

More specifically: market the moment someone wants the list.

Good post shapes:

- I made a list of baby shower gifts that are actually for the person recovering.
- A housewarming list that is not candles and cutting boards.
- A small-space baby registry that does not take over your apartment.
- Kitchen gifts for someone who cooks, not someone who performs cooking.
- A starter apartment list for people who want to buy fewer bad things.

Each post should link to the public collection first, not the homepage.

Avoid feature-led hooks:

- Save products from any store
- Organize your wishlist
- Create collections with budgets
- Track links in one place

Prefer outcome-led hooks:

- Send one list your family can actually use
- Stop showing up with another candle
- Give the new parent something that helps after everyone leaves
- Make the apartment list you wish someone had handed you
- Be ready when someone asks, "What should I buy?"

## Product Implications

Prioritize:

- Public collection polish
- Template browsing
- Copy/clone flow
- Clear authorship or curator identity
- Useful preview cards for shared links
- Internal links from use-case pages to public collections
- Search-indexable public collection pages

Do not prioritize yet:

- Affiliate dashboards
- Commission tracking
- Brand campaign workflows
- Heavy analytics
- Creator monetization language

## Competitor User Targeting

### Moonsift Users

Moonsift is the closest public comp — universal product saving, curation angle, browser extension. As of mid-2026 they have a small but real user base (1M+ products saved as of 2023) who are already shopping-list-aware. Their AI copilot is still in beta, their UX skews toward wishlists and registries, and their editorial/taste dimension is weak.

Their users are worth targeting because:

- They're already in the habit of saving products cross-retailer
- They likely feel the gap between "saved wishlist" and "something I'd actually send someone"
- Moonsift's aesthetic is functional, not opinionated — Tote's curation angle is a genuine differentiator

**Channels to reach them:**

- Reddit threads where Moonsift gets mentioned (r/ProductivityApps, r/LifeProTips, shopping/wishlist subreddits) — show up with a useful public collection, not a pitch
- Chrome Web Store listing — Moonsift users searching for alternatives or reading reviews
- App Store positioning — target same keywords (universal wishlist, product saving, shopping list)
- Direct comparison content: "Moonsift vs Tote" or "alternatives to Moonsift" — honest framing, lean into the taste/editorial difference
- If Moonsift ever winds down or raises again with noise, watch for community displacement moments

**Message angle for this audience:**

Don't lead with saving or organizing — they already have that. Lead with the curation/sharing outcome: the list you'd actually send, the edit that reveals taste, the thing you make for someone else rather than for yourself.

## Research Plan

Run a two-week practical tastemaker sprint.

### Build

- Publish 5-10 public collections from the priority list
- Add the strongest examples to `/templates`
- Link relevant use-case pages to the public collections
- Make sure each collection has a clean title, description, and preview image behavior

### Share

- Share public collection links directly with 15-25 people who fit the persona
- Post 3-5 list-led examples publicly
- Ask people which list they would actually send to someone else

### Measure

Track:

- Public collection views
- Template page views
- Clicks from use-case pages to public collections
- Signups from public collection and template pages
- Copy/clone starts
- Extension installs after public collection views
- Qualitative replies: "Can you make one for X?"

## Decision Rules

Double down when:

- People ask for more lists in the same style
- People send a collection to someone else
- A public collection gets direct/referral traffic
- A template produces signups or clone starts
- The title works as both a search phrase and a text someone would send a friend

Cut or rewrite when:

- The template is too generic to share
- The page sounds like SEO filler
- The list does not reveal taste
- The list requires too much explanation before it feels useful
- The topic attracts views but no copy/share behavior

## Working Mantra

Tote is not trying to become an affiliate platform first.

Tote is trying to become the cleanest place for practical tastemakers to make useful, shoppable lists from across the internet.
