Tote — Internal Strategy

Discoverability & Go-to-Market 2026

May 2026  ·  Pre-launch draft  ·  Updated 6 May 2026

Where we're starting from

The product is real and working — Chrome extension, web app, public sharing, AI curator. iOS is built and functional but currently dev-only (not yet on the App Store). Current user count is ~3 (internal only). That's not a problem; it's just the honest starting line. The strategy below is sequenced around that reality.

01

What the product already has going for it

A working product across three surfaces

Chrome extension, web app, and iOS (dev-only pending App Store launch). Save from anywhere, compare in one place, share a clean public URL. Most tools at this stage are one surface only.

Privacy-first positioning that's genuinely differentiated

No affiliate model, no tracking, no brand deal requirements. LikeToKnowIt and similar tools are built around monetizing the creator's audience. Tote is the opposite.

Public shareable collections — a native viral mechanic

Every shared Tote URL is a free ad. When someone sends tote.tools/s/daniel/a-montessori-nursery… to their sibling, the sibling sees a beautiful product board and learns what Tote is organically. The loop is already there.

An AI curator that can produce templates at scale

The curator was built partly to generate example collections. That makes it a content engine — the thing that feeds the templates library, use case pages, and SEO surface over time.

Clear use case density

Gift lists, home renovation, style boards, family shopping, professional sourcing. These aren't hypothetical — they're real search-intent categories with people actively looking for help.

02

The core discoverability problem

The home page and use case pages are well-crafted marketing. But they're a closed loop — no actual Tote content to browse. Every current entry point assumes someone already knows about Tote.

The /s/[username]/[slug] URLs are the actual value: real collections with real products, crawlable by Google, shareable by anyone. Right now they're invisible unless someone sends a direct link.

The underlying GTM question

How do you get more people sharing collections in the first place? Everything else should feed that flywheel.

04

The agentic shopping landscape — and where Tote fits

In late 2025 and into 2026, every major AI platform launched or announced shopping agents. This is relevant context for Tote's positioning — and it's mostly good news.

ChatGPT Instant Checkout (OpenAI) Pivoting

Launched in Sept 2025 with Etsy and Shopify. Powered by the Agentic Commerce Protocol (with Stripe). OpenAI subsequently scaled it back — onboarding merchants was arduous, and checkout was error-prone. Shifting focus to product discovery inside ChatGPT, letting merchants handle their own checkout. ~30 Shopify merchants made it live before the pivot.

Perplexity Agentic Shopping Free tier, search-first

Launched free to US users with PayPal integration and 5,000+ merchants. Search-first architecture means real-time pricing and availability. Zero fees/commissions to merchants (vs. ChatGPT's 4% ACP fee). Deep comparison is their core strength. Abandoned ads entirely in Feb 2026.

Google Shopping AI / UCP Platform-scale

Google's Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP) lets merchants plug into Google AI mode to create multi-item carts, real-time pricing, and auto-purchase when conditions are met (e.g. price drops). Gemini can instruct a purchase once price targets are hit. Operates at search-engine scale.

Amazon / Rufus Closed loop

Rufus is Amazon's shopping AI, optimized within the Amazon ecosystem. High capability for Amazon-sold products; no cross-store awareness. Classic closed-loop model.

Do any of these compare photos or product specs?

Perplexity does deep spec comparison well — it's research-grade. Google's Gemini can compare products using structured data and images when available. ChatGPT's vision can analyze product images if you paste them, but it's not automated into the shopping flow. None of them do visual similarity across stores in a meaningful way — e.g. "find me this chair but cheaper" from a photo is still weak across the board.

Where Tote is different from all of these

Every agentic shopping product above is built for the transaction moment — find something, buy it now. They're optimizing for conversion speed on behalf of the merchant.

Tote is built for the consideration phase — the days or weeks between "I saw something I want" and "I'm ready to decide." That's the messy middle none of the agents are designed for. They help you buy faster; Tote helps you buy better.

More importantly: all of the agentic tools are ephemeral. The conversation ends, the context is gone. Tote is persistent — your saved links, collections, and shortlists are there when you come back six weeks later.

The curator is the closest thing Tote has to an "agent" — AI helping you build a collection — but the output lives in your account, is shareable, and is a curated human-first artifact, not a transactional flow.

Risk to watch

As these agents get better at understanding intent and tracking down products, the "discovery" phase of shopping may compress. If ChatGPT can build you a curated list of Montessori nursery items in 30 seconds, the motivation to use a dedicated tool is lower — unless Tote's curation, persistence, and shareability create enough additional value. The templates + curator + shareable collections strategy is the right hedge against this.

05

Go-to-market strategy

Target audiences (start narrow)

The "anyone who shops online" framing is too broad for early GTM. Pick two to start, both of which have clear sharing behavior and specific search intent:

Gift givers & list makers

Clear seasonal peaks, high sharing behavior, strong search intent ("gift list for toddlers", "holiday gift ideas under $50").

Home renovators & furnishers

High-consideration purchases, strong motivation to compare across stores, natural budget-tracking need, often shared with a partner or contractor.

New parents (nursery, gear)

Extremely high research intensity, passionate community sharing, clear template opportunities, year-round demand.

Interior designers & stylists

Professional use case, highest willingness to pay, natural client-sharing workflow, strong word-of-mouth within industry communities.

Strategy pillars

1. Seasonal content marketing

Shopping behavior is deeply seasonal. Q4 (holiday gift lists) is the single biggest opportunity. Templates and use case content should be timed around back-to-school (Aug), holiday (Oct–Dec), home refresh (Jan–Feb), and spring projects (Mar–May). Start building Q4 content in late summer.

2. The "shoppable link in bio" angle

LTK requires affiliate relationships and brand deals. Tote's version: save anything from anywhere, share a clean board — no brand deals required. Interior design creators, mom bloggers, and style accounts who currently send Google Docs links to followers are the target. The pitch isn't "make money" — it's "stop sending messy links."

3. Community seeding

Before a public launch, get real collections into communities where they naturally fit — not promotional posts, genuinely useful contributions. A "Montessori Nursery Under $800" collection posted to r/BabyBumps with a thoughtful comment will get upvoted and shared. Same for r/InteriorDesign, r/femalefashionadvice, r/HomeImprovement. The Tote branding on the collection does the marketing.

4. Product Hunt launch (timed)

Not immediately. Do this after the templates page and public collection experience are polished. Goal is ecosystem awareness and a burst of early adopters, not viral growth. Works best when there's a clear "try it in 2 clicks" path.

5. Long-tail SEO via templates

Searches like "home renovation shopping list tracker", "baby registry checklist with prices", and "capsule wardrobe shopping list" are underserved by useful tooling. A well-structured templates page with real collections and good titles could realistically rank for some of these in 6–12 months.

06

The curator as content engine & freemium path

The AI curator isn't just an internal tool — it's the content engine that makes the templates strategy scalable without a manual curation team.

Curator infrastructure — May 2026

Recent work has meaningfully improved the curator's reliability and UX. Session history now reads from Neon (previously Jazz), so session titles — generated by a fast Haiku call early in the workflow — appear in the sidebar as soon as they're ready rather than only at completion. The tab-refocus reconnect ensures the brief-approval step is never missed when switching windows. The curator is ready to run at volume for template generation.

  • Curator generates high-quality template collections
  • Templates page attracts organic search traffic
  • Visitors clone templates → signup conversion
  • Users share their own collections publicly
  • Shared collections attract more visitors
  • Power users (designers, creators) upgrade for curator access
  • Their shared collections drive more discovery

The curator-as-upgrade-path is a clean freemium model because the free tier is genuinely useful on its own. The paid upgrade has a clear, demonstrable value: AI-assisted collection building that would otherwise take hours of manual research.

Where to draw the freemium line

Free: save links, clone templates, basic collections, basic public sharing.

Pro: curator access, unlimited public collections, advanced budget and shortlist features. Aimed at designers, stylists, content creators, and serious home planners.

One risk to watch

Curator-generated templates could erode the personal curation feel that makes Tote distinct. Templates should be a starting point — the user's own saves and decisions are still the product. Frame collections as "inspiration to clone and make your own," not "here's the answer." This is also the key differentiator from the ChatGPT/Perplexity shopping agents above.

Aim for 30–50 templates across use cases before the public launch push. Quantity matters for SEO coverage. They don't need to be perfect — they need to be genuinely useful enough that someone would clone them and build from there.

07

What to actually do first

The most important thing right now isn't discoverability

With ~3 users, the viral loop hasn't been tested in the wild yet. Before SEO, templates, or Product Hunt — get 10–20 real strangers using it. You need to know: do people outside the household find it useful? Do they share collections? Do they come back? Those answers should shape everything else.

The lowest-friction path: direct outreach to people you know who fit the use cases. A friend doing a home renovation. Someone who just had a baby. A designer you know. Not a cold launch — just "I built this, would you try it for your actual project?" Then listen hard.

What to avoid

  • Paid social before organic is working — Instagram/TikTok ads are expensive and signal is poor until you know your actual converting audience.
  • Press outreach too early — TechCrunch doesn't move the needle for consumer tools at this stage; niche communities and newsletters do.
  • Trying to serve everyone — "anyone who shops online" is too broad for early GTM. Pick two audiences and be specific.
  • Gating the freemium model before you understand usage patterns — with a small user base you can't yet tell which features are worth paying for.
  • Treating templates as AI-generated answers — they should be starting points, not finished products, to preserve the personal curation feel.
  • Competing head-on with ChatGPT/Perplexity on "find me a product" — they have more compute and distribution. Tote wins on persistence, curation, and shareability, not speed of discovery.
